  • 6 Myths About Seasonal Allergies

    Did you know that seasonal allergies affect more than 35 million Americans? But just because this sneezy misery is so common doesn't mean it's widely understood. See how many of these popular myths about allergies have fooled you.

  • Myth: People with allergies should stay away from flowers.

    Fact: The pollen from bright, showy flowers isn't usually to blame for seasonal allergies. Instead, it's the pollen from more humble-looking plants you need to watch out for. In most parts of the United States, that means trees in early spring, grasses in late spring to early summer, and weeds in late summer to fall.

  • Myth: Allergy
    season can’t get worse over time.

    Fact: Recent research suggests that spring is coming earlier these days, thanks to rising temperatures and carbon dioxide levels. And that means pollen season is starting sooner. Ragweed—a common cause of late summer to early fall allergies—is also growing faster and flowering earlier in some areas because of climate change.

  • Myth: Moving is a drastic solution to seasonal allergies, but it works.

    Fact: Don't move to another part of the country just to escape seasonal allergies. Many plants, especially grasses, are found across most parts of the United States. Plus, your body may just find new pollens to react to. Instead, it's much better to work with your doctor to avoid triggers and find treatments that keep your allergies under control.

  • Myth: Eating local honey protects you from pollen.

    Fact: Honey, local or not, won't save you from allergy symptoms. While honey does contain pollen spores, they're not the kind that trigger your allergies.

  • Myth: If you stay inside, you’re safe from allergies.

    Fact: Many allergens are found right inside your home, including molds, pollen, dust mites, and animal dander. Experts say that a central air conditioning system with a HEPA filter is useful for fighting these common culprits. Get rid of your carpets if you can, and wash all bedding once a week in hot water.

  • Myth: You can’t develop seasonal allergies as an adult.

    Fact: Just because you were allergy-free as a kid doesn't mean you'll remain so as an adult. And believing this myth can keep you from getting the treatment you need. If you're experiencing sneezing, congestion, a runny nose, and itchy eyes, talk to your doctor.
